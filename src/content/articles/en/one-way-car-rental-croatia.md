A one-way car rental from **Split to Dubrovnik** — picking up in one city and dropping in the other — is a popular way to drive the Dalmatian coast without doubling back. Expect a **one-way drop-off fee of roughly €100–€250** on top of the rental itself, and know that since the **Pelješac Bridge opened in 2022, the drive stays entirely inside Croatia** — no more Bosnia/Neum border stop, no passport needed for the route itself. One-way makes sense if Dubrovnik is your end point and you're flying out from there; if you're looping back to Split anyway, a normal return rental is cheaper. Fees below are planning ballparks in euros (€), not quotes — always confirm with the rental company when you book.

> **Heads-up.** Drop-off fees, insurance terms and cross-border rules vary by company and change over time, and the figures here are orientation, not a quote. Confirm the exact one-way fee, the green-card/cross-border policy and whether your route needs a passport when you book. Checked July 2026.

## What a one-way rental actually costs

The car-hire price is the same whether you keep the car in one city or move it; the extra you pay for one-way is the **drop-off fee** (sometimes called a "one-way fee" or "relocation fee"), charged because the company has to get the vehicle back to its home base.

- **Split → Dubrovnik specifically:** typically **€100–€250**, since it's a relatively short hop down the coast (about 230 km).
- **Longer routes** (say Dubrovnik to Zagreb, ~600 km): higher, usually **€200–€300**.
- **Same city, different branch** (e.g. Split town to Split airport): **usually free**.
- **Flat-fee policies exist:** some firms publish a fixed charge — Autowill, for example, advertises a flat **€250**; Hertz lists around **€300** to return to another location in Croatia. Others scale the fee by distance.

The fee is normally shown at the booking stage, so compare a few providers — for a short route like this one the gap between companies can be larger than the fee itself. Off-season, some offer reduced or waived one-way fees to reposition cars. ## The Pelješac Bridge vs the Neum corridor

This is the part that's changed, and old advice online is often out of date. There are two ways to drive Split–Dubrovnik, and they're no longer equal.

**The Pelješac Bridge route (the default now).** Since the **2.4 km Pelješac Bridge opened in July 2022**, the main road crosses the bay on Croatian soil and the journey is **about 236 km in roughly 3 hours 13 minutes with no border crossing at all**. There's **no toll on the bridge**. For a one-way rental this is the simple choice: you **don't need a passport** for the route, and there's no Bosnia paperwork to think about.

**The Neum corridor route (the old way).** Before the bridge, the coastal road dipped through a short strip of **Bosnia and Herzegovina at Neum**, splitting Croatia in two. That route is **about 229 km — 7 km shorter and, with no border queue, around 12 minutes faster**. But it **crosses two international borders**, so you need a **passport or EU ID card**, and the wait is a lottery: locals report **under two minutes off-season but up to two hours each way in peak summer**. Most drivers now take the bridge precisely to avoid that gamble.

**A 2026 caveat worth flagging:** renovation works on the Pelješac Bridge are scheduled to **start around October 2026 and run into May 2027**. A full closure isn't planned, but there may be disruption and possible diversions onto the old D8 — so if you're driving in that window, check the current status before you set off.

## Taking a hire car across a border (green card and insurance)

If you stick to the **bridge route, this section doesn't apply** — you never leave Croatia. It matters if you take the **Neum corridor** (into Bosnia) or plan a detour into **Montenegro** (for example, on to Kotor):

- **Tell the rental company first.** Most Croatian firms allow driving into Bosnia, Montenegro and Slovenia, but you usually need **written permission** and sometimes pay a small cross-border surcharge. Crossing without it can void your insurance.
- **Green Card.** Ask for the **Green Card** — the international motor-insurance certificate — listing the countries you'll enter. For Bosnia and Montenegro this is the document border police may ask to see.
- **One-way across a national border is different.** Dropping a Croatian car in another country (not just another Croatian city) is a separate, much pricier arrangement — cross-border drop-offs can run **over €400** and aren't offered by every company. The Split→Dubrovnik one-way is **domestic**, so it avoids all of this.
- **Toll roads still apply.** Even staying in Croatia, the fast inland stretch on the **A1 motorway** is tolled by distance; you pay by card or cash at the exit barrier. Budget a few euros for tolls on top of fuel — the Pelješac Bridge itself is free, but the motorway sections are not.

A common mistake is to assume the old Bosnia rules still bite on this route. They don't, as long as you take the bridge — the green-card and passport requirements above only matter if you deliberately route through Neum or carry on into Montenegro afterward.

## Is one-way worth it, or should you do a round trip?

It comes down to your route shape:

- **One-way wins if** Dubrovnik (or Split) is a genuine end point — you're flying out from there, continuing by ferry, or simply don't want to retrace 230 km. Paying €100–€250 to skip a five-hour round-trip drive back is usually worth it, and it frees you to take the scenic coastal road one way without thinking about the return.
- **A round trip wins if** you're based in one city and exploring out and back, or doing a loop that returns you near the pickup point — then there's no drop-off fee at all, and a same-city return is often free.
- **Either way, build the drive in:** the Split–Dubrovnik run is a highlight, not just a transfer. Stop in **Ston** for the walls and oysters, detour onto the **Pelješac wine road**, and you've turned the transfer into part of the trip.
